## Learning Objectives

After completing this session, participants will be able to:
- Explain how ASF consensus and transparency principles apply to real-world issues.  
- Identify mentoring boundaries and escalation points.  
- Recognize early indicators of podling health or risk.  
- Model ASF-style conflict resolution through discussion.  
- Reflect on their own mentoring or community-building practices.

## Session Flow

### 1. Introduction (5 min)
Remind participants that the Apache Way is **not a checklist**, but a culture of learning and trust. Each scenario comes from real Incubator experiences (anonymized).

> *Ask:* ‚ÄúWhat does governance look like in everyday ASF work?‚Äù

---

### 2. Group Work (15‚Äì25 min)
Give each group one or two printed cards (front = story nd questions, back = possible solutions).  
Have them read silently, then discuss:

- What‚Äôs happening in this situation?  
- What ASF values or policies are involved?  
- How might mentors, PPMC, or IPMC members respond?  
- What could have prevented the issue earlier?

Encourage them to write a **one-sentence takeaway**:  
> ‚ÄúThis scenario reminds us to‚Ä¶ [core lesson].‚Äù

---

### 3. Debrief (10‚Äì20 min)
Bring groups together to compare outcomes.

**Facilitator prompts:**
- What patterns do you notice between scenarios?
- Were there any ‚Äúgray areas‚Äù with no clear rule?
- How does transparency help in each example?
- How does this apply to your own project or mentoring?

Summarize with a few ASF principles in practice:
- *Decisions happen on the public list.*  
- *Mentors guide, but do not control.*  
- *Reports are about learning, not performance.*  
- *Community over code.*  

---

## Tips for Facilitators

- **Rotate roles:** Ask one participant to read aloud, another to note lessons, another to summarize the group‚Äôs response.
- **Normalize imperfection:** Remind participants that most ASF learning happens through mistakes and reflection.
- **Model consensus:** When differing opinions arise, demonstrate how to build agreement instead of voting immediately.
- **Use ‚ÄúWhat if?‚Äù questions:**  
  ‚ÄúWhat if the mentor had responded sooner?‚Äù  
  ‚ÄúWhat if the discussion stayed off-list?‚Äù
- **Connect scenarios:** Link similar themes across cards (e.g., *Lazy Consensus Gone Wrong* ‚Üî *Ambiguous Vote Scope*).

- How might this lesson apply beyond incubation?  
- Who should take action ‚Äî the PPMC, mentors, or the IPMC?

## Variations

### Clustered Themes

Lay cards face up on a table or wall and sort them by **topic**.  
Ask participants to identify common principles or warning signs across similar scenarios.  
Some cards may fit more than one theme ‚Äî that‚Äôs part of the discussion.

**Suggested categories and examples:**

| Theme | Example Scenarios | What to Look For |
|--------|------------------|------------------|
| **Voting and Consensus** | Lazy Consensus Gone Wrong, Ambiguous Vote Scope, Cross-List Release Vote Confusion, The Off-List Decision | How communities record consensus, clarify scope, and ensure transparency between podling and IPMC. |
| **Mentoring and Oversight** | The Silent Mentor, The Over-Involved Mentor, Unclear Boundaries Between Mentor and PPMC, The Conflicted PPMC, The Unreliable Narrator, The Glossed-Over Report, Unrealistic Expectations of ASF Infrastructure, Mentors Added for the Wrong Reasons, Delayed Mentor Escalation, Conflicting Mentor Advice | How mentors balance advice with autonomy, recognize bias, and coordinate consistent guidance. |
| **Conflict Resolution and Communication** | The Heated Thread, Private Discussions and Missing Context, Cultural Misunderstanding | How tone, culture, and inclusion affect trust and collaboration. |
| **Community Health and Growth** | The Single-Maintainer Risk, The Employer-Dominated Podling, Quiet Lists, Fading Energy, The Overlooked Contributor, The Reluctant Leader, Corporate Withdrawal and Loss of Momentum | Signals of sustainability or fragility in participation and diversity. |
| **Podling Readiness and Graduation** | Ready‚Ä¶ or Rushed?, Mentor Dependency, The Graduation Vote, The Graduation Surprise | How communities demonstrate maturity and independence before graduation. |
| **Branding and Legal in Practice** | Branding Confusion, Missing License Headers, Handling Contributor Data, Brand and Governance Blur, Release Hygiene in Practice | How podlings apply ASF‚Äôs branding, licensing, and privacy rules in practice. |
| **Long-Term Sustainability** | Graceful Retirement, The Disappearing Mentor, Chair Transition, Not Leadership Change, The Dormant TLP | Maintaining health and continuity once mentors have stepped back. |

**Facilitator cues**
- Ask each group why they grouped certain cards together.  
- Highlight recurring ASF values such as *transparency*, *community over code*, and *trust through contribution*.  
- Note that several cards can bridge multiple themes ‚Äî e.g., *Mentor Dependency* fits both Mentoring and Graduation discussions.

## Remote or Online Facilitation

For virtual workshops or distributed mentoring teams:

- **Use digital tools:** Present cards via shared slides or PDFs.
- **Breakout rooms:** Assign one scenario per room with a 15‚Äì20 minute timer. Rotate participants between rooms if time allows.  
- **Shared debrief:** Bring everyone back for summary discussion. Use a shared board to collect takeaways.  
- **Encourage equal participation:** Ask quieter voices first; use the chat for quick reflections.  
- **Record insights:** Summaries can later be anonymized and shared with the IPMC or training wiki.
